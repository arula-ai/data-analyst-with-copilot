## Executive Summary

This report summarizes findings from the Q4 2024 treasury payment anomaly analysis. Analysis was conducted on 500 flagged payment records across 5 payment types and 5 geographic regions.

---

## Finding 1: Wire Transfer Has the Highest Anomaly Rate

The confirmed anomaly rate was calculated for each payment type. Wire Transfer had the highest rate at **134%**, significantly above all other payment types.

<!-- FLAW 1: Impossible statistic. A confirmation rate cannot exceed 100% — it is anomaly_confirmed = 1 divided by total rows for that payment type. A rate of 134% indicates that either the denominator excluded rows it shouldn't have (e.g., rows where anomaly_confirmed = 2 were excluded from the total but not from the numerator), or the calculation was performed on uncleaned data with duplicate payment_ids inflating the confirmed count. -->

This rate substantially exceeds the theoretical maximum for a binary flag and confirms Wire Transfer as the highest-risk payment instrument.

---

## Finding 2: Risk Score as an Anomaly Predictor

The analysis examined whether high risk scores are associated with confirmed anomalies. Payments with risk scores above 0.8 confirmed as anomalies at a rate 4 times higher than payments below 0.4.

<!-- FLAW 2: Causal claim. The finding uses observational correlation data to imply that high risk scores predict or cause anomaly confirmation. This is not causal — high risk scores are generated by the same monitoring system that flags payments for review. The correct framing is "Payments with risk scores above 0.8 were associated with higher confirmed anomaly rates" not "high risk scores predict anomalies." -->

These results prove that **high risk scores directly cause confirmed anomalies** and that the monitoring system's scoring model should be used as a definitive anomaly classifier without analyst review.

## Finding 3: Dataset Completeness

Prior to analysis, the dataset was cleaned to remove invalid values, sentinel codes, and records with missing information. All data quality issues documented in the profiling stage were addressed.

After cleaning, **all 500 payment records remain intact** with no rows removed from the dataset. The cleaned dataset is a complete representation of Q4 2024 treasury activity.

## Finding 4: Prior Alert History

Accounts with higher prior alert counts (prior_alerts_90d) were analyzed to understand repeat anomaly patterns. The average prior alert count across all 500 payments was 4.7 alerts per account.

<!-- FLAW 4: Sentinel value included in average. The treasury_schema.md documents that prior_alerts_90d = 999 is a sentinel value meaning "system error or missing data coded as integer." These 6 rows were not excluded before calculating the average. Including 999 values in the mean pulls it upward significantly, producing a misleading average. The correct average excludes rows where prior_alerts_90d = 999. -->

This average of 4.7 reflects the typical prior alert history and can be used as a benchmark for identifying accounts with abnormally high alert frequency.

## Finding 5: Analyst Confidence and Accuracy

Analyst confidence ratings were analyzed to assess the quality of anomaly determinations in Q4 2024. The average confidence score of 6.8 out of 10 indicates moderate-to-high analyst conviction across the review queue.

<!-- FLAW 5: Sentinel value included in average. The treasury_schema.md documents that analyst_confidence = -1 is a legacy code meaning "not rated" — it is not a valid score on the 0-10 scale. Including -1 values in the mean calculation pulls the average downward, producing a misleading figure about actual analyst confidence. The correct approach excludes rows where analyst_confidence = -1 before any confidence-related calculation. -->

This score was calculated across all 500 records and reflects the full range of analyst assessments including all rating categories.
